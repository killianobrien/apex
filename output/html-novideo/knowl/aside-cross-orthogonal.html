Parallel vectors and the cross product.

We could rewrite Definition 11.3.11 and Theorem 11.4.5 to include \(\vec 0\text{,}\) then define that \(\vec u\) and \(\vec v\) are parallel if \(\vec u\times\vec v=\vec 0\text{.}\) Since \(\vec 0\cdot \vec v =0\) and \(\vec 0\times \vec v = \vec 0\text{,}\) this would mean that \(\vec 0\) is both parallel and orthogonal to all vectors. Apparent paradoxes such as this are not uncommon in mathematics and can be very useful. (See also the aside in Section 11.2.)
