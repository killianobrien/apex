Consider \(S_n\text{,}\) the \(n\)th partial sum.
\begin{align*} S_n \amp = a_1+a_2+a_3+\cdots+a_n\\ \amp = 1^2+2^2+3^2\cdots + n^2.\\ \end{align*}

By Theorem 5.3.7, this is

\begin{align*} \amp = \frac{n(n+1)(2n+1)}{6}\text{.} \end{align*}
Since \(\lim\limits_{n\to\infty}S_n = \infty\text{,}\) we conclude that the series \(\ds \infser n^2\) diverges. It is instructive to write \(\ds \infser n^2=\infty\) for this tells us how the series diverges: it grows without bound. A scatter plot of the sequences \(\{a_n\}\) and \(\{S_n\}\) is given in Figure 9.2.3.(a). The terms of \(\{a_n\}\) are growing, so the terms of the partial sums \(\{S_n\}\) are growing even faster, illustrating that the series diverges.
