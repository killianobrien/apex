Recall \(\ln 1= 0\) and \(\ln x\lt 0\) when \(0\lt x\lt 1\text{.}\) So \(\ln(1-\varepsilon)\) is negative because \(1-\varepsilon\lt 1\text{;}\) hence we consider its absolute value:
\begin{align*} \amp\abs{\ln(1-\varepsilon)}\\ \amp\quad =-\ln(1-\varepsilon)\\ \amp\quad =\ln\left(\frac{1}{1-\varepsilon}\right)\text{.} \end{align*}
To determine which is smaller between \(\abs{\ln(1-\varepsilon)}\) and \(\ln(1+\varepsilon)\) amounts to determining which is smaller between \(\frac{1}{1-\varepsilon}\) and \(1+\varepsilon\text{.}\) But
\begin{align*} \amp(1+\varepsilon)/\left(\frac{1}{1-\varepsilon}\right)\\ \amp\quad =(1+\varepsilon)(1-\varepsilon)\\ \amp\quad =1-\varepsilon^2\lt1\text{,} \end{align*}
so \((1+\varepsilon)\lt\frac{1}{1-\varepsilon}\text{.}\) And therefore \(\ln(1+\varepsilon)\lt\abs{\ln(1-\varepsilon)}\text{.}\)
