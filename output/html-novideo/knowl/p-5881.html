The first integral labeled \(a\) is easy to integrate. The \(\cos(2x)\) term is also easy to integrate, especially with Key IdeaÂ 6.1.4. The \(\cos^2(2x)\) term is another trigonometric integral with an even power, requiring the power-reducing formula again. The \(\cos^3(2x)\) term is a cosine function with an odd power, requiring a substitution as done before. We integrate each in turn below.
\begin{align*} \underbrace{\int\cos(2x)\, dx}_{b} \amp = \frac12\sin(2x)+C\\ \underbrace{\int\cos^2(2x)\, dx}_{c} \amp = \int \frac{1+\cos(4x)}2\, dx\\ \amp = \frac12\big(x+\frac14\sin(4x)\big)+C\text{.} \end{align*}
