Solution 2.4.5.1.

We first expand the expression for \(y\text{;}\) a little algebra shows that \(y = 2x^4+3x^3-6x^2+1\text{.}\) It is easy to compute \(y'\text{:}\)
\begin{equation*} y' = 8x^3+9x^2-12x\text{.} \end{equation*}
Instead, let's apply the Product Rule to the original factored form:
\begin{align*} y' \amp = \lzoo{x}{x^2+3x+1}(2x^2-3x+1)+(x^2+3x+1)\lzoo{x}{2x^2-3x+1}\\ \amp = (2x+3)(2x^2-3x+1)+(x^2+3x+1)(4x-3)\\ \amp = \left(4x^3-7x+3\right)+\left(4x^3+9x^2-5x-3\right)\\ \amp = 8x^3+9x^2-12x\text{.} \end{align*}
The uninformed usually assume that “the derivative of the product is the product of the derivatives.” Thus we are tempted to say that \(y' = (2x+3)(4x-3) = 8x^2+6x-9\text{.}\) Obviously this is not correct.
