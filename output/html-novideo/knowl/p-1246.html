When a rational function has a vertical asymptote at \(x=c\text{,}\) we can conclude that the denominator is \(0\) at \(x=c\text{.}\) However, just because the denominator is \(0\) at a certain point does not mean there is a vertical asymptote there. For instance, \(f(x)=(x^2-1)/(x-1)\) does not have a vertical asymptote at \(x=1\text{,}\) as shown in FigureÂ 1.6.11. While the denominator does get small near \(x=1\text{,}\) the numerator gets small too, matching the denominator step for step. In fact, factoring the numerator, we get
\begin{equation*} f(x)=\frac{(x-1)(x+1)}{x-1}\text{.} \end{equation*}
