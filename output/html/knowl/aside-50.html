The step where the left hand side of the differential equation condenses to the derivative of a product can feel a bit magical. The reality is that we choose \(\mu(x)\) so that we can get exactly this condensing behavior. It's not magic, it's math! If you're still skeptical, try using the Product Rule and Implicit Differentiation to evaluate \(\displaystyle \frac{d}{dx}\left (e^{-\frac{1}{2}x^2}y\right)\text{,}\) and verify that it becomes \(e^{-\frac{1}{2}x^2}\left(\displaystyle \frac{dy}{dx} - xy\right)\text{.}\)
