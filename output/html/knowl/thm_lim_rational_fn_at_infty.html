Theorem 1.6.21. Limits of Rational Functions at Infinity.

Let \(f(x)\) be a rational function of the following form:
\begin{equation*} f(x)=\frac{a_nx^n + a_{n-1}x^{n-1}+\dots + a_1x + a_0}{b_mx^m + b_{m-1}x^{m-1} + \dots + b_1x + b_0}\text{,} \end{equation*}
where \(m,n\) are positive integers and where any of the coefficients may be \(0\) except for \(a_n\) and \(b_m\text{.}\) Then:
  1. If \(n=m\text{,}\) then
    \begin{equation*} \lim_{x\to\infty} f(x) = \lim_{x\to-\infty} f(x) = \frac{a_n}{b_m}\text{.} \end{equation*}
  2. If \(n\lt m\text{,}\) then
    \begin{equation*} \lim_{x\to\infty} f(x) = \lim_{x\to-\infty} f(x) = 0\text{.} \end{equation*}
  3. If \(n \gt m\text{,}\) then \(\lim_{x\to\infty} f(x)\) and \(\lim_{x\to-\infty} f(x)\) are both infinite.
