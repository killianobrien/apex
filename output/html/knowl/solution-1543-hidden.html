Using trigonometry, \(\ell = x\tan(\theta)\text{,}\) so \(d\ell = \tan(\theta) dx + x\sec^2(\theta) d\theta\text{.}\) With \(\theta = 85^\circ\) and \(x=30\text{,}\) we have \(d\ell = 11.43dx+3949.38d\theta\text{.}\) The measured length of the wall is much more sensitive to errors in \(\theta\) than in \(x\text{.}\) While it can be difficult to compare sensitivities between measuring feet and measuring degrees (it is somewhat like “comparing apples to oranges”), here the coefficients are so different that the result is clear: a small error in degree has a much greater impact than a small error in distance.
