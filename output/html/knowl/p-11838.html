Since \(\vec d_1\) and \(\vec d_2\) are parallel, \(\overrightarrow{P_1P_2}\) lies in the plane formed by the two lines. Thus \(\overrightarrow{P_1P_2}\times\vec d_2\) is orthogonal to this plane, and \(\vec c=(\overrightarrow{P_1P_2}\times\vec d_2)\times \vec d_2\) is parallel to the plane, but still orthogonal to both \(\vec d_1\) and \(\vec d_2\text{.}\) We desire the length of the projection of \(\overrightarrow{P_1P_2}\) onto \(\vec c\text{,}\) which is what the formula provides.
