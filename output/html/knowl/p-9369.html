We can repeat this approximation process by creating polynomials of higher degree that match more of the derivatives of \(f\) at \(x=0\text{.}\) In general, a polynomial of degree \(n\) can be created to match the first \(n\) derivatives of \(f\text{.}\) Figure 9.7.4 shows \(p_4(x)= -x^4/2-x^3/6+x^2+x+2\text{,}\) whose first four derivatives at 0 match those of \(f\text{.}\) (Using the table in Table 9.7.3, start with \(p_4^{(4)}(x)=-12\) and solve the related initial-value problem.)
