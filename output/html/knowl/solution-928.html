Solution 9.8.6.1.
The derivative of \(\tan^{-1}(x)\) is \(1/(1+x^2)\text{.}\) Taking successive derivatives using the Quotient Rule, the derivatives of \(\tan^{-1}(x)\) fall into two categories in terms of their evaluation at \(x=0\text{.}\)
When \(n\) is even, \(\ds f^{(n)}(x) = (-1)^{(n-1)/2}\frac{p(x)}{(1+x^2)^n}\text{,}\) where \(p(x)\) is a polynomial such that \(p(0) = 0\text{.}\) Hence \(f^{(n)}(0) = 0\) when \(n\) is even.
When \(n\) is odd, \(\ds f^{(n)}(x) = (-1)^{(n-1)/2}\frac{p(x)}{(1+x^2)^n}\text{,}\) where \(p(x)\) is a polynomial such that \(p(0) = (n-1)!\text{.}\) Hence \(f^{(n)}(0) = (-1)^{(n-1)/2}(n-1)!\) when \(n\) is odd. (The unusual power of \((-1)\) is such that every other odd term is negative.)
The Taylor series starts \(x-\frac13x^3+\frac15x^5+\cdots\text{;}\) by reindexing to only obtain odd powers of \(x\text{,}\) we get
the Taylor series is \(\ds \infser[0] (-1)^n\frac{x^{2n+1}}{2n+1}\text{.}\)
