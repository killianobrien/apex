We begin by demonstrating that \(C\) lies on the surface \(z=6-x^2-y^2\text{.}\) We can parametrize the \(x\) and \(y\) components of \(C\) with \(x=\cos t+1\text{,}\) \(y=\sin t+1\) as before. Lifting these components to the surface \(f\) gives the \(z\) component as \(z = 6-x^2-y^2 = 6-(\cos t+1)^2-(\sin t+1)^2 = 3-2\cos t-2\sin t\text{,}\) which is the same \(z\) component as found in Example 15.7.13. Thus the curve \(C\) lies on the surface \(z=6-x^2-y^2\text{,}\) as illustrated in Figure 15.7.17.
Since \(C\) and \(\vec F\) are the same as in the previous example, we already know that \(\oint_C\vec F\cdot\, d\vec r = 3\pi\text{.}\) We confirm that this is also the value of \(\iint_\surfaceS (\curl\vec F)\cdot \vec n\, dS\text{.}\)
We parametrize \(\surfaceS\) with
\begin{equation*} \vec r(u,v) = \langle v\cos u+1,v\sin u+1, 6-(v\cos u+1)^2-(v\sin u+1)^2\rangle\text{,} \end{equation*}
where \(0\leq u\leq 2\pi\) and \(0\leq v\leq 1\text{,}\) and leave it to the reader to confirm that
\begin{equation*} \vec r_u\times \vec r_v = \la 2v\big(v\cos u+1\big), 2v\big(v\sin u+1\big),v\ra\text{,} \end{equation*}
which also conforms to the right-hand rule with regard to the orientation of \(C\text{.}\) With \(\curl \vec F = \langle 2y,0,-1\rangle\) as before, we have
\begin{align*} \amp\iint_\surfaceS (\curl\vec F)\cdot \vec n\, dS\\ \quad\quad\amp = \int_0^1\int_0^{2\pi} \la 2v\sin u+2,0,-1\ra\cdot \la 2v\big(v\cos u+1\big), 2v\big(v\sin u+1\big),v\ra\, du\, dv \\ \quad\quad\amp =3\pi\text{.} \end{align*}
Even though the surfaces used in this example and in Example 15.7.13 are very different, because they share the same boundary, Stokes' Theorem guarantees they have equal “sum of curls” across their respective surfaces.
